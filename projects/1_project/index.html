Wildfire Smoke Detection

AI-powered early detection system using advanced Vision-Language Models for environmental protection and disaster prevention.

San Diego Supercomputer Center 2024-2025 Research Team

Project Overview

This cutting-edge project represents a breakthrough in environmental AI, focusing on the early detection of wildfire smoke plumes through advanced computer vision and natural language processing techniques.

By leveraging state-of-the-art Vision-Language Models (VLMs) like Qwen2-VL, our system can analyze sequential satellite imagery and camera feeds to identify the earliest signs of wildfire ignition. The integration of computer vision with natural language processing enables the model to provide detailed analysis and early warnings, potentially saving lives and preventing extensive environmental damage.

Technology Stack

AI/ML Technologies
  • Qwen2-VL (Vision-Language Model)
  • Computer Vision Pipelines
  • Natural Language Processing
  • Real-time Image Processing
Data Sources
  • Satellite Imagery
  • Ground Camera Feeds
  • Weather Data Integration
  • Historical Fire Data

Key Features

Real-time Detection

Continuous monitoring of satellite and camera feeds for immediate smoke pattern recognition.

Natural Language Analysis

Advanced VLM integration for detailed textual analysis and contextual understanding of fire conditions.

Early Warning System

Automated alerts and notifications for emergency response teams and local authorities.

Impact & Applications

Environmental Protection

Early detection enables rapid response to prevent small fires from becoming catastrophic wildfires, protecting ecosystems and biodiversity.

Community Safety

Provides crucial early warning time for evacuation and emergency response, potentially saving lives and property.

Cost Reduction

Significantly reduces firefighting costs by enabling early intervention when fires are smaller and more manageable.

Climate Impact

Helps mitigate climate change by preventing large-scale wildfires that release massive amounts of CO2 into the atmosphere.
